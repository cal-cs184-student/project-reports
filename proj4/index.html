Altering Density and Spring Constant

Density

When the density is increased, the cloth becomes heavier and falls faster due to the force of gravity. It becomes more resistance to deformation as it falls on itself (as we can see on the top part of the cloth), making it more easy to create folds and wrinkles

Spring Constant

When ks value increased, the cloth becomes stiffer and more resistant to bending and stretching. This can make it more easier to create folds and wrinkles.

Part 5 - Shaders

Shader program provide an efficient way to rendering realistic material appearances without using exhaustive ray tracing. These programs consist two part: the vertex and the fragment shader. The vertex shader assigns attributes to the vertices in the scene and transforms the scene to normalized devices coordinates. The fragment shader assigns an RGBA vector to each pixel on the display, based on interpolated vertex data. By manipulating the vertex attributes in the fragment shader thus various material effects can be produced. Once the pixels value for each vertex are determined, the fragment shader interpolates between them to fill in the remaining pixels, since every vertex corresponds to a single pixel.

Bling-Phong Shading

  • Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screen shot only outputting the diffuse component, a screen shot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.

The Blinn-Phong shading model consist of three part: ambient, diffuse, and specular reflections. Ambient light is the constant intensity that uniformly reflects off all objects in the scene. Diffuse lighting depends on the surface normal of the object relative to the illumination source, with the highest intensities appearing on surface normals that face the light source. Specular reflection produces a glare that depends on the illumination source position, surface normal, and viewing position, and is characteristic of glossy materials.

Bump and Displacement Mapping

Displayed below are images comparing bump mapping and displacement mapping of a texture on a sphere. In the first row of images, the sphere's surface is a total of 16x16 vertices. Visually, bump mapping appears to outperform displacement mapping for this low sampling rate. The blocky appearance of the 16x16 displacement mapping image is noticeable due to the interpolation of physical height perturbations over the sphere from a relatively sparse number of samples. On the other hand, the texture itself has an abundant amount of high-frequency content over the sphere that cannot be accurately represented by the surface displacements resulting from the low sampling rate. In contrast, the second row of images features a higher sampling rate of 128x128 samples on the sphere. This higher sampling rate is adequate for the surface displacements to match the texture's high-frequency content. In this case, displacement mapping accurately depicts both the shading of the texture and the physical deformations on the sphere defined by the texture.
